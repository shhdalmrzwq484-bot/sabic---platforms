Preface

About This Handbook

This handbook was developed during my training experience with SABIC, within the Electrical & Power System Analysis / Engineering & Project Management environment, to serve as a practical reference for new engineers and trainees entering industrial electrical project engineering.

The idea behind this handbook came from a real need: when a new engineer joins an industrial projects team, the learning curve can be steep. Electrical design is not learned as isolated topics, but as a connected engineering workflow where each decision affects the next. A load schedule influences transformer sizing, transformer sizing affects switchgear selection, switchgear defines cable and protection requirements, and every part of the system must satisfy safety, reliability, standards, and project requirements.

For that reason, this handbook follows the actual sequence an engineer may encounter in an industrial electrical project. It begins with understanding loads and design inputs, then moves step by step through power distribution, transformers, switchgear, motors, cables, grounding, protection coordination, and hazardous-area classification.

The purpose of this handbook is not only to explain formulas, standards, or equipment definitions. Its main goal is to build engineering judgment: why each calculation matters, how design decisions are made, what common mistakes should be avoided, and how engineering choices appear in real project documents and industrial site conditions.

I created this guide to support trainees, fresh graduates, and junior engineers as they begin working with industrial power system design. My aim is to make the early learning journey clearer, more structured, and more connected to real engineering practice.

This handbook is intended to be a dependable companion for the first stages of an engineer's career — helping them understand the system, ask better questions, make safer decisions, and gradually grow from learning the design process to contributing confidently to real industrial projects.

This handbook is a working reference, not a textbook. It is organized to mirror the daily reality of an electrical engineer at SABIC: you are handed a load list, a plot plan, or a one-line, and you must arrive at a defendable, code-compliant, SES-aligned design. Chapter 1

Electrical Load Schedule and Calculation

Every electrical design decision in an industrial facility — transformer kVA, switchgear ratings, cable cross-sections, generator capacity, UPS autonomy — traces back to one controlled document: the electrical load schedule. This chapter establishes the terminology, category rules, and step-by-step calculation method that turn a raw process load list into a defensible design demand at every bus and voltage level.

Why it matters

An over-conservative schedule wastes capital and substation floor space; an aggressive one trips breakers at commissioning and forces unbudgeted retrofits. In a design review, every number on the schedule must be traceable to a source and a rule.

Learning objectives
  1. Distinguish demand, diversity, coincidence, utilisation, and load factors per IEEE Std 100 — and apply each at the correct aggregation level.
  2. Classify every load as continuous, intermittent, standby, or spare, and map IEC 60034-1 duty types to schedule treatment.
  3. Execute the eight-step reduction from shaft kW to design demand kVA, including VFD, redundancy, and harmonic flags.
  4. Aggregate per bus and per voltage level, and verify the result against the largest-motor starting transient.

1.1Purpose & Scope

The load schedule is the foundation document of the entire electrical design. Every downstream sizing calculation — transformer kVA, switchgear ratings, cable cross-sections, generator capacity, UPS sizing, harmonic filter design — flows from the numbers in this single spreadsheet. A schedule that is too conservative wastes capital and substation floor space; one that is too aggressive trips breakers in commissioning and forces unbudgeted retrofits. The goal is a defensible, auditable estimate of connected load, demand load, and future margin — at every bus, at every voltage level, in every operating mode.

This chapter is structured around five things that must be true of every issued load schedule:

  1. Terminology matches IEEE Std 100 — diversity, demand, coincidence, and load factors are not interchangeable.
  2. The connected-to-demand reduction is applied at the right level — within a homogeneous category, between dissimilar categories on a bus, between buses on a substation.
  3. Special load types (VFD, UPS, lighting, heat tracing, redundant pumping) are treated by category-specific rules, not by a single global multiplier.
  4. Each load is tagged to a bus and a voltage level — substation transformers are sized from per-voltage demand, not plant-total demand.
  5. The schedule is a controlled document with revision history — every number must be defensible against its source.

Scope note
This chapter covers the load schedule as a SABIC-internal deliverable. Where SAES (Saudi Aramco) interface requirements apply, the differences are flagged inline.

1.2Engineering TerminologyGet this right first

The single most common defect in engineering-school-trained load schedules is loose terminology. The factors below are formally defined in IEEE Std 100 (Standard Dictionary of Electrical and Electronics Terms) and IEC 60050.

Key formulas

DFdem = Pdem,max / Pconn
DivF = Σ Pdem,max,i / Pdem,max,system
CF = 1 / DivF
LF = Pavg / Ppeak

This handbook uses CF at the bus level (when summing dissimilar load categories) and DF at the unit level (when reducing an individual load below its connected rating). Reserve DivF for citing IEEE Std 100 directly; do not use it as a numerical multiplier on the schedule.

Engineering Note
Why terminology matters
A design office that labels its multiplier "diversity factor 0.85" and uses it as a ≤ 1 demand-side reduction is not numerically wrong; the term has been misused in industrial practice for fifty years. But in a SABIC design review the inspector may ask, "is this the IEEE diversity factor or the IEC ks coincidence factor?" — and the correct answer must be on the schedule. Write demand factor or coincidence factor explicitly.

1.3Load Categories & Duty Types

Every load on the schedule is assigned exactly one category, which drives its demand treatment and its inclusion in emergency / UPS subtotals.

Continuous

Operate ≥ 3 hours at a stretch. Process pumps, blowers, HVAC, lighting, process heaters. IEC duty type S1. Sized at 100% of input kVA at the unit level; coincidence applies at the bus level.

Intermittent

Operate < 3 hours per cycle, typically IEC duty S3 (intermittent periodic) or S6 (continuous operation with intermittent load). Mixers, sample pumps, intermittent compressors, conveyors with idle periods. Demand factor 0.30 – 0.70 depending on cycle duration factor (CDF).

Standby

Operate only on emergency or process upset. Firewater pumps, EDG-fed loads, emergency lighting on UPS. DF = 1.00 with no diversity — by definition these run when needed, and the design contingency is that they all run together.

Spare

Future capacity allocated to specific bus positions in the MCC or switchgear. Typically 20% of connected load on each LV bus and 10% on each MV bus per SES practice. Listed as a single allocation line per bus, not multiplied by diversity.

A motor labelled S3 40% is not equivalent to an S1 motor of the same shaft kW. On the schedule, use the input kVA at the S1-equivalent average load (= nameplate kVA × CDF).

1.4Standard Calculation Method

Every load on the schedule passes through a five-stage reduction from shaft kW to design demand kVA. Figure 1.1 below summarises the sequence; each step is then documented in detail.

For each load on the schedule, follow this sequence. Every column on the spreadsheet maps to a step.

  1. Step 1 — Establish shaft kW. The process/mechanical engineer provides the shaft (mechanical) kW or BHP for each pump, compressor, fan, and mixer. For heaters, lights, and resistive loads, the nameplate kW is already electrical input — skip Step 2.
  2. Step 2 — Convert shaft kW to electrical input kVA. Apply motor efficiency (η at full load, from IEC 60034-30-1 IE class) and full-load power factor (PF, from motor data sheet).
Pelectrical = Pshaft / η
kVAunit = Pshaft / (η × PF)

For VFD-fed motors, include drive efficiency (ηdrive ≈ 0.97) and input PF (≈ 0.95 with line reactor, ≈ 0.98 with AFE):

kVAinput = Pshaft / (ηmotor × ηdrive × PFinput)
  1. Step 3 — Apply utilisation factor within the unit. A pump rated 75 kW but operating at duty point 60 kW has ku = 0.80. Operating kVA per unit = kVAunit × ku. For loads designed at duty point, ku = 1.00.
  2. Step 4 — Apply redundancy / running quantity. For 2 × 100% redundant pumps (1 duty + 1 standby), running quantity = 1. For 3 × 50% parallel pumps, running quantity = 2. The transformer is sized from the running set; cable and starter for each unit are sized for a single unit at rated.
  3. Step 5 — Sum within each category, per bus, per voltage level. Group by Bus ID, Voltage, and Category. Use Excel SUMIFS so the aggregation is auditable: =SUMIFS(kVA_running, Bus, "MCC-01", Voltage, 480, Category, "Continuous").
  4. Step 6 — Apply coincidence factor at the bus level. Between dissimilar categories on the same bus, CF = 0.85 (continuous-process), 0.80 (mixed industrial), 0.70 – 0.80 (utility / HVAC dominated). For emergency and UPS buses, CF = 1.00. Within a homogeneous category of continuously-running units, do not apply coincidence — those loads run together by design.
  5. Step 7 — Add future margin. Typical 20% of bus demand per SES practice. Important regional note: Saudi Aramco SAES-P-100 §7.6 mandates sizing at 110% of operating-plus-known-future loads — equivalent to a 10% margin on top of an already-explicit future tag list. Confirm the project policy before applying 20%.
  6. Step 8 — Verify against largest-motor starting. Transformer impedance and impulse capability must support the largest single motor's starting kVA (typically 6 × FLA for DOL, see Chapter 5) in addition to the running demand of the bus. If the resulting voltage dip exceeds 15% at the motor terminals, escalate to soft-start or VFD.

The output is the design demand kVA per voltage level, which feeds Chapter 3 (transformer sizing) and Chapter 4 (switchgear sizing).

1.5Motor & Driver Special Loads

Motor-fed loads (rotating equipment, VFDs, redundant pump sets) account for 70 – 90% of demand on a typical petrochemical bus. Treat them by the rules below; do not use a global diversity factor.

1.5.1 VFD-fed motors

A variable-frequency drive interposes three things between the motor shaft and the AC bus:

  • Drive losses: ηdrive ≈ 0.96 – 0.98 across the operating range. Size the input kVA at the drive-output kW divided by drive efficiency.
  • Input power factor: 6-pulse diode rectifier ≈ 0.95 (with line reactor); 12-pulse ≈ 0.96; active front end (AFE) ≈ 0.98 – 0.99.
  • Harmonic content: 6-pulse drives inject 5th, 7th, 11th, 13th harmonics into the bus. This does not increase kVA, but it derates the upstream transformer.

For the load schedule, compute the input kVA at full motor load and flag the row as a harmonic load. When cumulative VFD load on a transformer exceeds ~ 25% of its capacity, apply transformer derating per IEEE C57.110-2018:

Derating = 1 / √( 1 + PEC-R × K / (1 + PEC-R) )

A typical 6-pulse bus has K ≈ 7 – 14, giving derating 0.80 – 0.85.

Engineering Note
VFD harmonic basis
Do not use IEEE 519 for transformer sizing — IEEE 519 sets harmonic limits at the PCC, not transformer derating. Use IEEE C57.110 for transformer K-factor.

1.5.2 Redundant equipment

The single most common defect in load schedules is counting both pumps in a 2 × 100% service. Rules:

ConfigurationRunning quantitySchedule treatment
2 × 100% (1 duty + 1 standby)1Both listed; only duty in running column
3 × 50% (2 duty + 1 standby)2All three listed; running = 2
N + 1 (N duty + 1 standby)NAll listed; running = N
2 × 100% both running (rare)2Both in running column

The cable and starter for each unit are still sized for one unit running at rated. The transformer is sized for the running set plus the inrush of one starting unit (per IEEE 3002.7).

1.5.3 Soft-started / wound-rotor motors

Soft-start and slip-ring motors draw the same steady-state kVA as DOL equivalents — the difference is only in the starting transient. For the load schedule, treat them identically to DOL motors at the same FL kW; the reduced starting kVA is a Chapter 5 (Motor Starting) consideration, not a schedule entry.

1.6Utility, Auxiliary & Allocated Loads

Non-rotating loads (UPS systems, lighting, heat tracing, resistive heaters, DC chargers) follow category-specific rules. Each is documented below.

1.6.1 UPS and battery chargers

A UPS appears on the AC schedule as a single rectifier / charger input. Two operating modes set the worst-case AC demand:

  • Steady state: AC input = (UPS output kVA / ηUPS) ≈ UPS kVA / 0.92.
  • Battery recharge boost: AC input = UPS output + battery recharge demand. For 8-hour autonomy batteries recharging at 0.1 C, recharge demand ≈ 0.20 – 0.30 × UPS rating.
AC feeder size ≈ 1.25 × UPSrated, kVA

List the UPS as a continuous load on the AC side; the downstream UPS loads (DCS, ESD, instrument) are tracked on a separate UPS-only sub-schedule that feeds Chapter 7.

1.6.2 Lighting and receptacles

  • Process area lighting: 100% continuous at unity DF. Typical 15 – 25 W/m² for outdoor process areas.
  • Office and control room lighting: 100% continuous.
  • Receptacles per NEC 220.44: first 10 kVA at 100%, remainder at 50%. Welding sockets in petrochemical maintenance areas (32 A 3-phase): demand factor 0.30, intermittent category.

1.6.3 Heat tracing

  • Maintain-temperature mode: typical demand factor 0.30 – 0.50 averaged over a season.
  • Start-up / first-energization: full load, all circuits energized simultaneously.

Size the heat-trace panel for first-energization; apply demand factor 0.50 for the upstream MCC contribution. Heat trace is normally a separate sub-schedule (one tag per heat-trace panel) feeding the MCC.

1.6.4 Process heaters (resistive / SCR)

Resistive process heaters (immersion, in-line, electrode boilers): 100% at unity demand, treated as continuous when in normal operation. SCR-controlled heaters: flag as harmonic load — phase-angle control injects 3rd, 5th, 7th harmonics. On a heater-heavy bus, the harmonic flag drives the transformer K-factor calculation.

1.6.5 Spare allocation

A blanket spare allocation per bus is standard SES practice: typically 20% of LV connected and 10% of MV connected. List as a single line per bus and do not apply diversity — it represents unknown future loads at unity demand.

1.6.6 DC loads

DC loads (control, switchgear closing/tripping, emergency lighting) draw from chargers that appear on the AC schedule. The DC load itself is tracked on a separate DC schedule feeding battery sizing in Chapter 7. The AC contribution is the rectifier input kVA at steady state plus 25% recharge margin, as for UPS.

1.7Per-Bus and Per-Voltage Aggregation

A multi-voltage SABIC plant typically has 33 kV or 13.8 kV utility incomer, 13.8 / 4.16 kV MV distribution, 480 / 400 V LV distribution, 240 / 120 V control, and 110 V or 24 V DC (UPS / battery-fed).

Each voltage level has its own transformer feeding it, and each must see the correct per-voltage demand. The load schedule must explicitly split by voltage:

  1. Tag every load with a Voltage column.
  2. Aggregate per Bus × Voltage × Category using SUMIFS.
  3. Apply coincidence at the bus level.
  4. Sum bus demands at each voltage to size the upstream transformer.

For a 4.16 kV / 480 V step-down transformer, the LV-side bus demand drives the kVA; for the 13.8 kV / 4.16 kV transformer above it, the demand is the sum of MV-direct loads plus the LV demand stepped up through transformer losses (≈ 2% at full load).

Design Check
Sanity check
The sum of LV-bus demands plus MV-direct demands should approximate the upstream transformer demand, within transformer losses and motor-starting transient. If it doesn't, there is an accounting error somewhere in the schedule.

Within each homogeneous category (continuous pumps), all units run, ku and redundancy are applied per unit, and there is no within-category coincidence reduction. 1.10Design PitfallsGrouped by failure mode

The mistakes below appear in design reviews with predictable frequency, grouped by the underlying class of error. Each card lists the failures and the rule that catches them.

Common MistakeA · Unit & quantity errors
  • Mixing kW (shaft / mechanical) and kVA (electrical) in the same total — always convert to kVA before summing. Maintain a separate column for each.
  • Sizing on FLA without the 1.25 multiplier required by NEC 430.22 for continuous motor loads — undersizes by 25% on every motor feeder.
  • Using VFD output kW as bus load. The input kVA loads the upstream transformer; VFD output is on the motor side only.
Common MistakeB · Diversity & coincidence misuse
  • Applying coincidence to safety-critical loads (firewater pumps, ESD valve actuators, emergency lighting on UPS). All sized at unity; diversity has no place on emergency or UPS buses.
  • Calling the bus-level multiplier "diversity factor". Use demand factor or coincidence factor for ≤ 1 multipliers; reserve diversity factor for the ≥ 1 ratio per IEEE Std 100. See §1.2.
  • Using Load Factor in the sizing chain. LF is an energy / tariff metric; it has no place in equipment sizing.
Common MistakeC · Harmonic & non-linear loads
  • Forgetting harmonic loads (VFDs, UPS rectifiers, SCR heaters). Each contributes harmonic kVA that derates the upstream transformer. Apply IEEE C57.110-2018 K-factor; do not cite IEEE 519 for this (it sets PCC limits only).
  • Specifying only THD-V without also checking individual harmonic limits — IEEE 519 requires both.
  • Treating SCR-controlled heaters as pure resistive loads. Phase-angle control injects 3rd, 5th, 7th harmonics; flag them. See §1.6.
Common MistakeD · Redundancy & starting
  • Counting both pumps in 2 × 100% service. Only the duty unit appears in the running quantity; counting both doubles the transformer. See §1.5.
  • Forgetting motor starting kVA separately — the schedule shows running demand; the transformer must additionally support the largest single motor inrush (Chapter 5).
  • Forgetting UPS recharge boost — size the AC input at ~1.25 × UPS rated kVA to cover charger plus battery recharge. See §1.6.
Common MistakeE · Regional & site-specific
  • Single global future margin. SES typically uses 20%; Saudi Aramco SAES-P-100 mandates the 110% rule (≈ 10%). Confirm project policy.
  • Saudi 50°C ambient derate omitted. Affects motor service factor, cable ampacity, and transformer rating by 5 – 10% each; apply explicitly downstream.
  • Treating space heaters as continuous — most operate only during shutdown or low ambient; use intermittent sizing with a winter-peak coincidence.

2.1Purpose & Core Philosophy

The Single Line Diagram (SLD) is not merely an administrative drawing or a schematic block layout; it is the definitive technical and contractual baseline of an industrial power system's architecture. It condenses a complex three-phase network into a standardised single-line format, providing an immediate overview of power flow paths, voltage boundaries, and protective configurations.

Every dynamic engineering calculation, protective device coordination study, short-circuit simulation, arc-flash hazard analysis, and mandatory safety Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) isolation routine depends entirely on the topographical accuracy of the SLD.

An authoritative, project-grade SLD must answer four critical questions at a glance for any field operator or senior electrical engineer:

  1. Source provenance — Where does the electrical energy originate? (Utility grid ties, captive on-site co-generation, or emergency diesel backup.)
  2. Distribution topology — How does power cascade hierarchically through the facility? (HV switchyard → MV switchgear → LV MCCs → localised lighting and control panels.)
  3. Protection & control boundaries — What safeguards the assets during a network anomaly? (Mapping of protective relays, instrument transformers, breakers, fuses, and surge arrestors.)
  4. Isolation & earthing boundaries — How is a sector safely de-energised for maintenance? (Air-break disconnectors, draw-out breaker trucks, and integral earthing switches required to establish a verified zero-energy state.)

2.3Reading an SLD — Step-by-StepThe diagram as a continuous flowchart story

To master the interpretation of a SABIC-grade SLD, do not look at it as isolated graphical shapes. Read it as a continuous flowchart story following electrical energy from the extreme upstream utility transmission down to the final downstream process motor. Figure 2.2 below is the canonical reference — keep it in view as you read through the six steps.

Step 1 · Source arrival — upstream utility connection

Look at the absolute top of the drawing sheet. You will see an incoming transmission line or outdoor switchyard connection at a high voltage profile (typically 115 kV or 230 kV within SABIC heavy-industrial zones), supplied by the regional utility (SEC). At this entry point, look for revenue-grade billing energy meters and HV surge arrestors designed to divert lightning surges away from station transformers.

Step 2 · Main guard — the incomer circuit breaker (ANSI 52)

Directly underneath the source, the energy encounters the first line of defence: the Incomer Circuit Breaker (ANSI 52). On MV/HV designs this is a Vacuum Circuit Breaker (VCB) or SF₆ breaker. On a catastrophic internal fault, this guard opens its contacts in milliseconds to decouple the plant from the national grid, preventing a plant fault from cascading into a regional blackout.

Step 3 · Step-down — the power transformer

Transmission voltages are excellent for long-distance transport but unusable for industrial machinery. The power line flows into the Power Transformer symbol, where voltage is stepped down (e.g., 115 kV → 13.8 kV). Look immediately below the secondary winding to identify the neutral earthing method — an explicit resistor symbol denotes a High-Resistance Grounding (HRG) system, used to limit single-phase-to-earth fault currents.

Step 4 · Distribution highway — the main switchgear busbar

Now operating at a manageable medium voltage (13.8 kV), the path runs into a thick horizontal line stretching across the page: the Main Switchgear Busbar. Power enters via the incomer and splits into multiple parallel paths called Outgoing Feeders. To maintain availability, SABIC SLDs use a split-bus architecture (Bus A and Bus B) divided by a central Tie Breaker — if Transformer A fails, the tie closes to allow Transformer B to keep both halves running.

Step 5 · The invisible observers — CTs, VTs, and relays

Look closely around the incoming and outgoing breakers. You will see small nested circles drawn around the lines: these are Current Transformers (CTs). They step thousands of primary amperes down to 5 A or 1 A secondary, feeding the numerical protection relay. If the relay detects overcurrent (ANSI 51) or instantaneous short-circuit (ANSI 50), it fires a trip command to the breaker mechanism. See §1.7 for the per-bus aggregation that drives CT ratio selection.

Step 6 · The destination — Motor Control Centers

The story terminates at the bottom of the diagram. Outgoing lines cascade through localised step-down transformers (13.8 kV → 415 V / 480 V) into Motor Control Centers (MCCs). Lines terminate at individual motor starter cells, heavy process extruders, chemical mixing grids, and critical plant automation panels that directly generate the physical output of the factory.

3.1Core Definitions & Operating FundamentalsPrimary, secondary, core, turns ratio

A power transformer is a static electromagnetic-induction machine that transfers electrical energy between two or more coupled circuits at a constant network frequency. It steps voltage up to optimise transmission efficiency or down to match downstream equipment utilisation thresholds. Four physical constituents carry the entire operating principle.

Primary winding

The excitation winding connected directly to the upstream power-supply network. It absorbs electrical energy from the source and establishes the alternating magnetic flux within the shared laminated core.

Secondary winding

The output winding connected to the downstream distribution busbar or motor control centres. Electrical energy is induced here by electromagnetic coupling — there is no galvanic connection between the primary and secondary circuits in a two-winding transformer.

Magnetic core

A high-permeability structural skeleton constructed from cold-rolled grain-oriented (CRGO) silicon-steel laminations. The laminations are assembled with step-lap joints to provide a low-reluctance path for the alternating flux while minimising internal hysteresis and eddy-current losses. The thickness of each lamination is typically 0.23 – 0.35 mm; modern designs use 0.18 mm laminations to reduce eddy-current losses further.

Turns ratio (a)

The mathematical scaling factor of voltage transformation, defined by the physical loop configuration of the coils:

a = N1/N2 = V1/V2 = I2/I1

Where N1 and N2 are the physical wire turns of the primary and secondary coils respectively, V is the terminal voltage, and I is the line current. Current scales inversely to voltage — a step-down transformer that reduces voltage by a factor of 30 multiplies current by the same factor on the secondary side, which is why distribution-transformer secondary cables are physically much larger than the primary cables.

Engineering Note
Per-unit (pu) note
In ETAP and IEC 60909 calculations, turns ratio disappears when all quantities are expressed in per-unit on a common MVA base. The transformer becomes a series impedance only. See Chapter 10 §10.2 for modelling conventions.

3.3Performance EquationsShort-circuit, regulation, and efficiency

Three closed-form equations govern the steady-state performance of every two-winding transformer. They are the input to short-circuit studies (Chapter 10), to motor-starting voltage-dip checks (Chapter 5), and to lifetime energy-loss calculations.

Symmetrical short-circuit fault current (Isc)

The maximum symmetrical short-circuit current available on the transformer secondary terminal during a solid three-phase, zero-impedance fault is set by the transformer impedance alone (utility source assumed infinite for first-pass sizing):

Isc = Irated / (%Z / 100) = ( Sbase × 100 ) / ( √3 × Vline × %Z )

Where Irated is the nominal full-load current, Sbase is the rated transformer volt-amperes (VA), Vline is the secondary line-to-line voltage, and %Z is the percent impedance (see §3.6). The formula assumes infinite source impedance; in practice the actual fault current is reduced by the upstream system impedance and increased by the contribution of running motors. Use IEC 60909 for the full network calculation.

Voltage regulation (VR%)

The percentage change in secondary terminal voltage when the load transfers from no-load to full-rated load:

VR% = ( Vno_load − Vfull_load ) / Vfull_load × 100

For inductive loads (industrial motor grids at PF ≈ 0.85 lag), the regulation drop is closely approximated using the winding resistance (R) and leakage reactance (X) expressed in percent:

VR% ≈ R%·cos(θ) + X%·sin(θ)

Where cos(θ) is the operating power factor of the aggregate plant load. Typical values for distribution transformers: R% ≈ 1.0 – 1.5%, X% ≈ 5.5 – 7.5%, giving VR ≈ 3 – 5% at full-load 0.85 PF.

Efficiency (η) and loss split

Efficiency must account for two physically distinct loss components: fixed core losses (no-load — hysteresis and eddy currents in the laminations) and variable copper losses (load — I²R heating in the winding resistance).

η = Pout / ( Pout + Pcore + Pcopper )
η = S·cos(θ) / [ S·cos(θ) + Pno_load + (Iactual/Irated)² · Pload_rated ]

Peak efficiency occurs at the load level where variable copper losses equal fixed core losses — typically at 40 – 60% of rated load for distribution transformers. This is why oversized transformers run inefficiently in the long term: a 2500 kVA transformer loaded continuously at 30% sits in its high-loss zone for life.

Engineering Note
Sizing for energy life-cycle cost
Procurement cost is paid once; loss energy is paid every operating hour for 30+ years. For continuously-loaded distribution transformers, a 1% improvement in efficiency at the duty-point loading repays the procurement premium for higher IE-class designs within 4 – 6 years.

3.4Sizing Logic

Transformer sizing is the bridge between the load schedule and the rest of the system. The selected nameplate kVA must (a) supply the design demand with margin, (b) survive the largest motor start without violating bus voltage limits, (c) provide acceptable short-circuit contribution for downstream device sizing, and (d) match the cooling class to ambient conditions and load factor.

The base sizing formula is:

Required kVA = Design Demand × (1 + Future Margin) / (Diversity × Cooling Derate × Temperature Derate)

After computing the required kVA, the engineer selects the next standard rating from the IEC 60076 ladder: 25, 50, 100, 160, 200, 250, 315, 400, 500, 630, 800, 1000, 1250, 1600, 2000, 2500, 3150, 4000, 5000, 6300, 8000, 10000 kVA.

3.6Impedance Voltage (%Z)

Impedance voltage governs two competing requirements:

  • Low %Z → less voltage regulation, better motor starting, but higher short-circuit contribution (and larger downstream switchgear ratings).
  • High %Z → higher voltage regulation, harder motor starts, but lower short-circuit current (smaller downstream gear).

Typical values per IEC 60076: 4.0% for ≤ 630 kVA, 6.0% for 800–2500 kVA, 6.25–8.0% for 3150–10000 kVA. SES often specifies the upper end of these ranges to limit downstream fault levels — verify the project specification.

3.7Vector Group

Vector group encodes (a) the connection of each winding (Y, D, Z) and (b) the phase displacement between primary and secondary. The most common groups in SABIC service are:

  • Dyn11 — most common power transformer for distribution. Delta primary blocks zero-sequence current; star secondary provides neutral for grounding.
  • Dyn1 — same as Dyn11 but with a 30° lag instead of lead. Used where coordination with existing system requires it.
  • YNyn0 — used for autotransformers and some special cases; both windings star-grounded.
  • YNd1 / YNd11 — generator step-up; star primary for grounding, delta secondary blocks third harmonic.

Two transformers paralleled must have identical vector groups, identical or very close %Z (within 10%), and identical voltage ratios (within 0.5%).

3.8Tap ChangerOLTC vs DETC · anatomy · control · failure forensics

Every distribution transformer is fed by a source whose voltage drifts above or below nominal — utility variations during peak load, generator excitation changes, or seasonal network shifts. The tap changer is the mechanism that physically alters the transformer turns ratio in discrete steps to keep the secondary bus voltage within ±5 % of nominal regardless of these upstream variations. It is the only moving element inside a transformer with significant duty cycle, and statistically the most failure-prone subsystem.

Orientation
Section at a glance
The OLTC is the only loaded moving element inside a power transformer — and therefore the most failure-prone subsystem. This section walks the principle (turns-ratio equation), the hardware (selector + diverter + transition impedance), the control logic (AVR with deadband, time-delay, and Line-Drop Compensation), three worked examples with full numbers, and the three failure modes that account for ~40 % of all transformer outages.

Working principle

The underlying physics is the transformer turns-ratio equation from §3.1 — voltage scales linearly with the ratio of secondary to primary turns:

VLV = VHV × ( NLV / NHV )

If VHV drifts above or below nominal because of utility variations, VLV drifts proportionally — unless we change the ratio. The tap changer physically inserts or removes turns from one winding (usually the HV side) to alter the ratio (NLV / NHV) and restore VLV to target.

Effective ratio at tap n: an = a0 × ( 1 + n × Δ )

Where a0 is the nominal ratio, n is the tap position (positive or negative integer), and Δ is the step size in pu (typically 0.0125 = 1.25 %). By convention, negative tap positions REMOVE turns from the HV winding — reducing the effective ratio and raising VLV. Positive taps add HV turns — increasing the ratio and lowering VLV. "Raise" on the AVR therefore commands a movement toward a more negative tap number.

OLTC vs DETC — selection criterion

Tap changers are classified by whether they can operate with the transformer energised and loaded:

  • On-Load Tap Changer (OLTC) — motor-driven mechanism that operates under full load and rated voltage. Standard for transformers ≥ 2 500 kVA serving voltage-sensitive loads (process heaters, MV motors, paralleled distribution feeders). Typical duty: 5 000 – 30 000 operations per year on an active regulating substation.
  • De-Energised Tap Changer (DETC) / Off-Circuit Tap Changer (OCTC) — manual hand-wheel selector. The transformer must be isolated and de-energised before any tap change (typical specification ±2 × 2.5 %, giving 5 positions). Acceptable for smaller units ≤ 1 600 kVA feeding stable loads, where the tap is set once at commissioning and changes only seasonally or with load growth.

OLTC internal anatomy

An OLTC is not a single switch — it is a coordinated assembly of two switches operating in sequence, plus a transition impedance that handles the brief instant when the load current bridges between two adjacent taps. The five components below must all appear in the procurement specification.

  • Selector switch — slow-moving rotary contacts that pre-select the next tap position while the load current still flows through the current position. No arcing here because the selector only moves when its contacts are unloaded.
  • Diverter switch — the fast-acting element that breaks and makes the actual load current during the transition between taps. This is where all arcing happens. The diverter is driven by a spring-loaded snap mechanism to minimise arc duration — typically < 50 ms total transit time.
  • Transition impedance — a resistor (or reactor in HV designs) inserted between the old and new taps during the diverter operation. Without it, the brief short-circuit between adjacent taps would generate destructive circulating currents through the regulating winding.
  • Motor drive mechanism — a small (typically 0.5 – 1.5 kW) AC motor with planetary gearbox that mechanically operates the selector and triggers the diverter spring. Includes end-of-travel limit switches, an electrical position transmitter, and an emergency hand-crank that must electrically lock out the motor when engaged.
  • Control cabinet — houses the Automatic Voltage Regulator (AVR) relay, position indicator (raise / lower / running), local raise–lower controls, parallel-control signalling, and remote SCADA interface.

Control modes

  • Manual — operator pushes raise / lower buttons on the OLTC cabinet. Used during commissioning, factory tests, and emergency situations where AVR is bypassed.
  • Automatic AVR — a voltage-sensing relay continuously compares the LV bus voltage against a setpoint. If the deviation persists beyond a deadband and time delay, the AVR issues a raise or lower pulse to the OLTC drive. The deadband (typically ±1.5 %) and time delay (30 – 120 s) prevent the OLTC from chasing brief transients and exhausting its contact life on noise.
  • Line-Drop Compensation (LDC) — the AVR setpoint is offset to regulate not the transformer terminals but an estimated remote-bus voltage, compensating for cable I·R and I·X drop under varying load. R-comp and X-comp constants are entered into the AVR based on the feeder impedance to the regulated point.
  • Parallel control — when two OLTC transformers feed a common bus, both must move together to avoid circulating reactive current. Two control philosophies are standard: Master–Follower (one AVR leads, the other slaves to the same tap position) and Circulating-Current Minimisation (each AVR independently senses bus reactive current and corrects). See §3.4 — paralleling also requires matching vector groups, voltage ratios, and impedances.
WORKED EXAMPLE 1AVR step response to a source-voltage sag

Transformer T-101, 2 MVA, 13.8 / 0.415 kV Dyn1, %Z = 6, OLTC on HV side, range ±10 % in 17 positions (Δ = 1.25 % per step), AVR deadband ±1.5 %, time delay 60 s, nominal tap 0. A neighbouring large-motor start sags the utility voltage from 13.80 kV to 13.40 kV (a −2.9 % step).

Nominal turns ratio: a0 = 13.80 / 0.415 = 33.25

WORKED EXAMPLE 2Circulating current from mistuned parallel OLTCs

Two identical 2 MVA, 13.8 / 0.415 kV transformers (T-A, T-B) with %Z = 6 % each feed a common 415 V bus carrying 1 500 kVA at PF = 0.85. An operator manually shifts T-A to tap −1 (raises its LV by 1.25 %) while T-B remains at tap 0.

Open-circuit LV from T-A: VA = 415 × 1.0125 = 420.2 V
Open-circuit LV from T-B: VB = 415 V

The two secondaries are tied at the bus, so they cannot actually differ — the 5.2 V open-circuit mismatch drives a circulating current through the loop A → bus → B → back via the neutral path. Solving in per-unit on the 2 MVA base:

ΔV (driving voltage): 0.0125 pu
Zloop = ZA + ZB = 0.06 + 0.06 = 0.12 pu
Icirc = ΔV / Zloop = 0.0125 / 0.12 = 0.104 pu
IFLA = S / (√3 × VLL) = 2 000 000 / (√3 × 415) = 2 783 A
Icirc = 0.104 × 2 783 = 290 A

290 A flows continuously through both transformer windings doing no useful work — it generates I²R copper losses and accelerates thermal aging of the insulation in both units. The reactive component also worsens the upstream PF and steals capacity that could be serving real load. This is the engineering case for mandatory parallel control: master–follower keeps both AVRs locked to the same tap position; circulating-current minimisation drives the loop current independently toward zero.

WORKED EXAMPLE 3Line-Drop Compensation in action

Distribution transformer feeds an MCC located 200 m away via a 3-core LV cable with impedance Z = 0.05 + j 0.04 Ω one-way at operating temperature. MCC load varies from 100 A overnight minimum to 800 A peak production. The target voltage is 415 V at the MCC terminals, not at the transformer terminals.

Without LDC — AVR regulates the transformer LV terminals to a fixed 415 V:

Light load  (I = 100 A,  PF = 0.85):
   cable drop  =  I × (R·cos φ + X·sin φ)
              =  100 × (0.05 × 0.85 + 0.04 × 0.527)

   cable drop  =  800 × 0.0636  =  50.9 V

The MCC sees only 364 V at peak load — far below the 415 V the downstream motors need to develop rated torque. With LDC enabled, Rcomp = 0.05 and Xcomp = 0.04 are entered into the AVR. The AVR computes a synthetic remote-bus voltage and regulates that instead of the transformer terminals:

Vremote, synthetic = Vtransformer − I · ( Rcomp·cos φ + Xcomp·sin φ )

The AVR now raises the transformer terminal voltage automatically as load current rises. At peak load, the AVR drives the transformer to roughly 466 V (subject to OLTC range) so that after the 51 V cable drop, the MCC still receives 415 V. The transformer-terminal voltage swings widely, but the regulated point — the actual MCC bus — stays within ±1.5 % across the full load range.

Failure forensics — three dominant mechanisms

OLTC contacts mechanically move thousands of times under load over the asset's lifetime, while the main-tank windings remain stationary. This duty cycle imbalance is why the OLTC is statistically responsible for 30 – 40 % of all power-transformer outages in industrial service. Three failure modes account for the majority.

Common Mistake1 · Pyrolytic carbon buildup on contacts (coking)
  • Mechanism: Every diverter arcing event thermally cracks the surrounding insulating oil, depositing pyrolytic carbon onto the contact surfaces and into the OLTC oil compartment.
  • Progression: Carbon accumulates on the contacts → contact resistance rises → I²R heating accelerates further carbonisation → contact temperature climbs at full load. Once contact resistance exceeds approximately 200 µΩ, the contact runs hot enough at rated current to ignite a thermal runaway.
  • Detection: Routine DGA on the OLTC oil compartment (separate sampling point from the main-tank oil — never mix). Acetylene presence indicates active arcing fault; methane / ethane indicates thermal degradation of the oil.
  • Mitigation: Scheduled OLTC oil filtration and replacement; contact-resistance measurement at every major inspection. Vacuum-type OLTCs eliminate this mechanism entirely by sealing the contacts away from oil.
Common Mistake2 · Drive mechanism failures / runaway tap changer
  • Mechanism: A stuck motor-drive contactor, failed limit switch, seized planetary gearbox, or broken coupling shaft can leave the OLTC mid-stroke or drive it continuously toward an end stop.
  • Consequence: Stuck mid-stroke = the transition impedance carries the full load current continuously → resistor overheats within seconds → oil fire risk. Driving past the end stop = mechanical destruction of the tap selector and gearbox.
  • Mitigation: Independent watchdog timer in the AVR control circuit that cuts motor power if a single tap operation exceeds the expected transit time. End-of-travel position transmitters wired to a high-priority control-room alarm. Annual no-load operation test through the full ±8-step range with motor-current monitoring.
Common Mistake3 · Selector contact wear & misalignment
  • Mechanism: Repeated indexing slowly wears the spring-loaded contact fingers; thermal cycling shifts the alignment of the fixed tap terminals on the regulating winding bus.
  • Consequence: Increased contact resistance → localised overheating at the affected position only. In extreme cases the selector fails to fully engage on one phase and the transformer loses a phase under load.
  • Mitigation: Contact-resistance measurement with a micro-ohmmeter at every major OLTC inspection (typical pass/fail threshold 100 µΩ per finger). Visual inspection of all contact fingers and fixed terminals; spring-pressure verification.
Field Note
Operations counter — the cheapest condition-monitoring tool
Every OLTC drive mechanism includes a mechanical operations counter on the cabinet. The manufacturer publishes a recommended inspection schedule keyed to the counter (typical: internal inspection at 30 000 operations, contact replacement at 100 000, gearbox overhaul at 200 000). Read and log the counter monthly — a sudden acceleration in operations rate signals an AVR hunting fault, abnormal upstream voltage instability, or a stuck-in-motion drive. Catching this in the first month of acceleration prevents what otherwise becomes a complete contact replacement six months later.

3.9Fault Anatomy — Electrical, Mechanical, ThermalHow transformers actually fail in the field

Section 3.13 lists the most common design-stage pitfalls in transformer specification. This section goes one layer deeper, into the physical failure mechanisms that occur in service. Understanding these mechanisms is what separates a routine specification from a defensible asset-protection design.

A · Electrical faults & insulation breakdown

Common MistakeTurn-to-turn fault (micro-level degradation)
  • Mechanism: Copper conductors are wound thousands of times and insulated turn-from-turn by kraft paper or enamel submerged in oil. Localised thermal degradation or transient surges fail the insulation between two adjacent turns, creating a shorted micro-loop.
  • Progression: The transformer action induces a massive circulating fault current inside the shorted turn, generating extreme local heat. If not cleared, the localised boiling destroys surrounding insulation and the fault rapidly evolves into phase-to-phase or phase-to-earth.
  • Detection: 87T differential protection (Chapter 9) is the primary protection. DGA (§3.15) catches it earlier — hydrogen evolution from low-energy partial discharges is the first marker.
Common MistakePhase-to-earth (ground) fault — dielectric failure
  • Mechanism: Breakdown of insulation between an active winding phase and grounded structure (steel core or outer tank). On a solidly-earthed system, this results in extreme fault current magnitudes per §3.12.
  • Mitigation: Modern industrial distribution uses High-Resistance Grounding (HRG) or Neutral-Grounding Resistors (NGR) on the secondary Wye neutral to limit single-phase-to-earth fault current to a safe profile — typically 10 A on MV networks and up to 400 A on LV. This prevents the catastrophic melting of transformer steel laminations during an internal ground fault.
Common MistakePhase-to-phase fault — bushing flashover
  • Mechanism: Direct short-circuit between two separate active phase windings. Extremely rare inside the main tank thanks to rigid structural phase barriers and wide mechanical spacing.
  • Where it actually happens: Outside the tank, at the HV bushing terminations. Causes: lightning flashover, severe airborne salt / dust pollution buildup on bushing porcelain, or wildlife (birds and snakes climbing across phases). Surge arrestors at the bushing termination and routine bushing washing are the standard defences.

B · Mechanical faults & electrodynamic stress

Common MistakeWinding deformation from through-faults (Lorentz forces)
  • Mechanism: When a massive short-circuit occurs far downstream in the plant, the transformer experiences a severe overcurrent surge passing through its windings. This through-fault generates intense instantaneous electrodynamic (Lorentz) forces acting both axially and radially. Forces can exceed tens of tons.
  • Damage: Repeated exposure to through-faults mechanically warps the wooden clamping blocks, bends copper conductors, and shifts winding axes. Once deformed, the windings lose their rigidity and collapse during subsequent normal load cycles. See §3.14 for the radial / axial force vector breakdown.
Common MistakeCore lamination slacking — magnetostriction & loose tie-bolts
  • Mechanism: The magnetic core is built from thousands of thin laminations clamped by heavy structural tie-bolts. The alternating magnetic field causes magnetostriction — physical micro-vibrations that produce the characteristic 100 / 120 Hz humming noise.
  • Progression: Over decades, the tie-bolts can loosen. Loose laminations vibrate against each other, abrading their interlaminar insulation coating. This creates shorted loops across the steel, giving rise to intense circulating eddy-current hot spots that scorch the surrounding oil. DGA picks this up via methane and ethane evolution.

C · Thermal & insulation degradation

Common MistakeLocalised hot spots
  • Mechanism: Unbalanced phase loading, uneven core flux distribution, or partial clogging of radiator cooling tubes. A small area inside the winding insulation reaches critical temperatures (> 140 °C) while the bulk oil temperature gauge still reads normal.
  • Progression: Localised baking transforms kraft paper into carbon and combustible hydrogen gas, drastically reducing its dielectric strength. The hot spot becomes a future flashover initiation point. DGA detects CO and CO₂ (cellulose degradation) and H₂ (corona).
Common MistakeMoisture & dielectric contamination
  • Mechanism: Transformers breathe ambient air through a desiccant breather as the oil expands and contracts with load cycling. If the silica-gel desiccant is oversaturated or unmaintained, humid air enters the oil reservoir.
  • Consequence: Insulation oil absorbs ambient moisture, causing a sharp drop in breakdown voltage (dielectric strength). When moisture droplets align under intense electric fields near HV windings, a conductive path forms — a catastrophic internal flashover follows. The silica-gel breather colour change (blue / pink) is the simplest in-service inspection check.

3.10Microscopic Failure PhysicsDP loss, Lorentz vectoring, oil oxidation chemistry

To audit a field failure or to evaluate the residual life of an aged unit, the design engineer must investigate the physical and chemical changes occurring at the atomic and molecular layers during a breakdown. The three mechanisms below dominate transformer end-of-life.

1 · Winding insulation pyrolysis (DP loss)

The structural health of solid insulation is measured by the Degree of Polymerization (DP) of the cellulose base paper — the average chain length of glucose units in the molecule.

  • New, factory-fresh insulation paper: DP between 1000 and 1200 glucose units.
  • Continuous thermal overloading breaks the molecular chains via acid-catalysed hydrolysis and pyrolysis. Each cycle of overload shortens the chains a little more.
  • Once the DP index drops below 200, the paper has lost its mechanical tensile strength entirely. It is as brittle as ash and shatters at the slightest mechanical vibration or through-fault magnetic squeeze — causing an instantaneous short-circuit.
Engineering Note
DP = 200 is the de facto end-of-life metric
DP cannot be measured non-invasively. The standard surrogate is the furan content in the oil (2-furfural, 2-FAL) measured during routine oil sampling. A 2-FAL > 5 ppm correlates strongly with DP < 250 and triggers a planned-replacement review per IEEE C57.91.

2 · Electrodynamic Lorentz force vectoring

During a short-circuit, peak currents produce mechanical stresses that grow as the square of peak current (Ipeak²). These stresses decompose into two destructive force vectors with distinct geometric directions:

  • Radial forces act outward on the outer HV winding (hoop stress — like trying to burst a pressure vessel from inside) while simultaneously forcing the inner LV winding inward against the core (buckling / collapse).
  • Axial forces act vertically up and down, trying to crush the winding ends against the upper and lower wooden clamping structures. If the magnetic centres of the primary and secondary coils are even slightly misaligned (from manufacturing tolerance or previous through-faults), axial forces try to rip the entire coil stack out of its clamps.

Through-fault current withstand is qualified per IEC 60076-5. A transformer that has survived a through-fault should be visually inspected at the next outage and screened by DGA — micro-displacement that doesn't cause an immediate failure still degrades the winding rigidity for future events.

3 · Interfacial oil oxidation & sludge formation

Mineral oil degrades over time when exposed to elevated temperatures and trace amounts of dissolved oxygen. The chemistry follows a predictable cascade:

  1. Heat + dissolved O₂ → polar compounds, organic acids, peroxides.
  2. Organic acids attack cellulose paper, accelerating its breakdown (feedback into DP loss above).
  3. Final byproduct: thick, sticky, dark carbonised sludge.
  4. Sludge settles heavily on winding cooling ducts and radiator interiors, acting as an insulating blanket that blocks oil flow and traps heat inside the windings.
  5. Trapped heat raises winding temperature → accelerates oxidation → more sludge. The thermal-runaway feedback loop is established.

Routine oil sampling (Interfacial Tension test, acidity, dielectric strength) per IEC 60422 catches this trend years before it causes a forced outage. Oil regeneration or replacement is significantly cheaper than transformer replacement.

1 · Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA)

Often called the "blood test" of a transformer, DGA is the most powerful predictive diagnostic method available. Any internal thermal, mechanical, or electrical fault thermally cracks the surrounding hydrocarbon oil and cellulose insulation molecules. A single sample tells you the present gas levels, not the rate of generation. Two samples 30 days apart, with the trend slope, are far more diagnostic than a single sample. A transformer with H₂ = 200 ppm steady for years is healthier than one rising from 50 to 150 ppm in 30 days.

2 · Buchholz relay (ANSI 63)

A mechanical protection device installed on the pipe connecting the main transformer tank to the elevated oil-conservator reservoir. It detects internal faults by mechanical effects rather than electrical ones, complementing the differential relay (87T).

  • Slow gas accumulation → alarm state. An incubation-stage fault (loose core bolt, minor turn-to-turn) releases small gas bubbles that rise toward the conservator and become trapped inside the Buchholz chamber. The trapped gas lowers the oil level inside the chamber and tilts a float-actuated switch, triggering a low-level alarm in the control room.
  • Severe shock wave → trip state. A catastrophic short-circuit or winding-to-ground arc instantly vaporises the surrounding oil, creating a high-pressure hydraulic shock wave. This shock wave rushes through the pipe toward the conservator and strikes an internal mechanical paddle, triggering a master high-speed trip command. Both primary and secondary breakers open in milliseconds, preventing structural tank rupture.

Tripping speed is in the order of 50 – 100 ms — comparable to differential protection. The two are complementary: Buchholz protects against slow-evolving and mechanical faults that may not produce a current differential; 87T protects against fast electrical faults that may not produce enough gas to trip Buchholz.

Switchgear and MCC Design

Switchgear and motor control centers are the distribution backbone that must carry normal current for decades and survive the worst fault the system can deliver. This chapter covers the equipment categories, the sizing parameters that appear on every data sheet, internal arc classification, and forms of internal separation.

Why it matters

Switchgear is unforgiving of specification errors: an interrupting rating exceeded by motor back-EMF contribution, or an arc classification below the bus fault level, converts a clearable fault into a catastrophic equipment failure with personnel exposure.

Learning objectives
  1. Match LV, MV, and HV switchgear categories to their governing IEC standards.
  2. Size continuous current, breaking/making capacity, and insulation coordination correctly.
  3. Specify internal arc classification (IAC) and form of separation per SES practice.

4.1Switchgear Categories

  • LV switchgear: ≤ 1000 V AC. Air circuit breakers (ACB) for the main, molded-case (MCCB) or air for feeders. IEC 61439-2.
  • MV switchgear: 1 – 36 kV. Vacuum or SF₆ circuit breakers, metal-clad construction with drawout breakers. IEC 62271-200.
  • HV switchgear: > 36 kV. Outdoor air-insulated (AIS) or gas-insulated (GIS). IEC 62271-203.
  • MCC (motor control center): low-voltage panel optimized for motor starters, with bucket-style drawout starters. IEC 61439-2.

4.2Key Sizing Parameters

Continuous Current Rating (Ir)

Sized for the maximum demand current of the bus, with a typical margin of 25% above the calculated demand for distribution buses and 50% for motor control centers (to account for starting transients on individual feeders).

Short-Circuit Current Rating

Switchgear must be rated for both the symmetrical short-circuit current (Isc, kA RMS) and the peak (asymmetric) value (Ip = √2 × κ × Isc, where κ is the asymmetry factor depending on X/R ratio). The breaking capacity (Icu / Ics) is the symmetric value the breaker can interrupt; the making capacity (Icm) is the peak it can close into. Standard ratings: 16, 25, 31.5, 40, 50, 65, 80, 100 kA.

Insulation Coordination

Specified by rated voltage (Ur), rated power-frequency withstand (Ud, 1-minute test), and rated lightning impulse withstand (Up, BIL). For 12 kV: Ur=12 kV, Ud=28 kV, Up=75 kV. For 36 kV: Ur=36 kV, Ud=70 kV, Up=170 kV.

4.3Internal Arc Classification

Per IEC 62271-200 Annex A, MV switchgear is tested for internal arc fault containment. Classification:

  • IAC-A: Authorized personnel access (front only).
  • IAC-B: Public access (limited, all sides).
  • IAC-FLR: Front, lateral, rear access — full SABIC standard practice.

Arc duration is typically 0.5 s (with arc-flash quenching) or 1.0 s (without). Specify arc rating in kA at the busbar SC level for the full 1-second duration unless arc-quench technology is approved.

4.4Form of Internal Separation

Per IEC 61439-2 (LV) and IEC 62271-200 (MV), internal separation forms range from Form 1 (no separation) to Form 4b (compartmentalized terminals). SES practice for MCCs is typically Form 3b or Form 4a; for MV switchgear, metal-clad construction (equivalent to Form 4) is mandatory.

4.5Common Pitfalls

  • Specifying breaking capacity without confirming making capacity is also adequate — a 40 kA breaker rated for X/R = 14 may not handle X/R = 20.
  • Forgetting motor contribution to the SC current — running motors contribute approximately 4–6× their FLA to a downstream fault for 3–5 cycles.
  • Underspecifying the busbar continuous rating — busbar must carry the sum of all simultaneously-loaded feeders, not the largest single feeder.
  • Mismatched IP rating — IP4X for indoor electrical room, IP54 for outdoor or process areas; confirm with HVAC and area classification.
  • Not accounting for cable termination space — MV switchgear with 630 mm² cables needs deeper rear compartments; verify before procurement.

Motor Design and Selection

Motors consume 70–90% of the demand on a typical petrochemical bus, and the moment of starting — not running — is where most electrical problems concentrate. This chapter covers selection criteria, the six starting methods and their kVA/torque trade-offs, the voltage-dip criterion, and motor cable and protection sizing.

Why it matters

A motor that starts cleanly on the test bench can collapse a weak plant bus. The starting method, source stiffness, and protection settings must be engineered together, or the plant learns the lesson during commissioning.

Learning objectives
  1. Select motors by load type, duty cycle, efficiency class, ingress protection, and hazardous-area certification.
  2. Compare DOL, star-delta, autotransformer, soft-start, and VFD starting on kVA, torque, and voltage dip.
  3. Apply the voltage-dip criterion (≤10% motor terminals / ≤15% bus / ≤20% transient) and the standard remedies.
  4. Size motor cables and protection per NEC 430 / IEC 60364 without nuisance-tripping on locked-rotor current.

5.1Motor Selection Criteria

  • Mechanical load type (centrifugal pump, reciprocating compressor, conveyor) — defines the speed-torque curve required.
  • Service factor (1.0 for severe duty, 1.15 for general purpose) — IEC defines this differently than NEMA; verify per project.
  • Duty cycle (S1 continuous, S2 short-time, S3 intermittent periodic) — IEC 60034-1.
  • Efficiency class (IE2, IE3, IE4) — IE3 minimum for ≥ 0.75 kW per IEC 60034-30-1; SES typically requires IE3 or IE4.
  • Ingress protection (IP55 outdoor process, IP54 indoor) — IEC 60529.
  • Hazardous area certification (Ex e, Ex d, Ex nA) — see Chapter 11.
  • Insulation class (F with B rise is standard for industrial service).

5.3Voltage Dip Criterion

During motor starting, the bus voltage drops because the source impedance is finite. The dip is approximately:

ΔV (pu) = Starting kVA / (Source SC MVA × 1000 + Starting kVA)

Acceptable dip values per IEEE 141 / SES practice:

  • ≤ 10% at the motor terminals (general practice for trouble-free start).
  • ≤ 15% at upstream MCC bus (running motors should not drop out).
  • ≤ 20% transient maximum (lighting and electronic loads should not drop out).

If the calculated dip exceeds these limits, options are: (1) use a reduced-voltage starting method, (2) increase the source size, (3) decrease the source impedance, or (4) negotiate with operations on starting sequence (only one large motor at a time).

5.4Cable and Protection Sizing for Motors

Per NEC Article 430 / IEC 60364:

  • Cable ampacity ≥ 1.25 × FLA for continuous-duty motors.
  • Short-circuit protection set to allow LRA without tripping (typically 13× FLA for inverse-time breakers, instantaneous setting at 1300% FLA).
  • Overload protection set to 1.15 × FLA for service factor 1.15 motors, 1.25 × FLA for SF 1.0.
  • Ground fault protection per IEEE 242 — typically zero-sequence CT with 50G/51G relay at 5–10% FLA.

  • Sizing the motor to exactly match the load kW — always allow 10–15% margin for process upset and aging.

Chapter 6

Emergency Diesel Generator (EDG)

The emergency diesel generator exists for one scenario: the utility is gone and the safety-critical loads must run. Sizing it is dominated not by steady-state kW but by the transient kVA of the largest block load — usually the firewater pump start. This chapter covers the sizing method, ISO 8528 rating classes, and excitation/voltage-recovery requirements.

Why it matters

An EDG that carries the running load but sags below ISO 8528 limits during block loading will trip on undervoltage exactly when the plant needs it most. The transient case is the design case.

Learning objectives
  1. Build the emergency load sequence and compute per-block transient kVA.
  2. Select the correct ISO 8528 rating class (ESP for SABIC emergency service) with site ambient/altitude derates.
  3. Specify PMG excitation and voltage-recovery performance for protection coordination.

6.1Purpose and Sizing Philosophy

The EDG provides power to safety-critical loads when the utility supply fails. Sizing is driven by two requirements: (a) the running kW load it must sustain after all loads are sequenced on, and (b) the transient kVA it must deliver during the largest single block load (usually the firewater pump start). The transient requirement frequently dictates the genset size, not the steady-state kW.

6.2Sizing Method

  1. Tabulate all emergency loads from the load schedule (DF = 1.00, no diversity).
  2. Identify the load sequence: which block starts first, second, etc.
  3. For each block, calculate the worst-case transient kVA the genset sees while that block starts (running load already on the bus + starting kVA of the new block).
  4. Compute steady-state kW = sum of all running emergency loads / overall PF (typically 0.8 lagging).
  5. Compute transient kVA = max of the per-block transient calculations.
  6. Select genset such that: (a) prime kW rating ≥ steady-state kW, and (b) transient kVA capability ≥ calculated transient kVA. Typical genset transient capability is 2.5–3× prime kVA for one cycle.

PMG (permanent magnet generator) excitation maintains short-circuit current at 3× rated for several seconds, allowing protection to trip on the faulted feeder rather than collapse the bus.   • Sizing on steady-state kW only — the genset trips on transient undervoltage during firewater pump start.
  • Forgetting fuel consumption and tank sizing — minimum 24 hr of full-load fuel per SES typical practice.
  • Ignoring exhaust back-pressure limits — long exhaust runs require oversized piping and silencers.
  • Specifying without PMG and then losing protection coordination during fault.
  • Forgetting cold-start requirements — block heaters, battery heaters, and fuel polishing are not optional in the desert / cold climate.

UPS and Battery Systems

UPS and battery systems keep the control system, ESD, and operator visibility alive through every disturbance the plant can throw at them. This chapter covers the IEC 62040 topologies, the sizing sequence from load tabulation to battery string design, battery chemistry selection, and the safety requirements of the battery room itself.

Why it matters

A DCS blackout of even a few hundred milliseconds can trip an entire process unit. The UPS is sized not for convenience but for the plant's ability to shut down — or ride through — safely.

Learning objectives
  1. Select the UPS topology (VFI online double-conversion is the SES default for process loads).
  2. Execute the six-step sizing sequence including autonomy time and IEEE 485/1188 battery sizing.
  3. Choose battery chemistry by autonomy, environment, and maintenance regime.
  4. Specify battery-room ventilation, containment, and safety provisions.

7.1UPS Topologies

  • Online double-conversion (VFI per IEC 62040-3): rectifier-battery-inverter; load is always fed from the inverter. SABIC standard for safety-critical and process control loads.
  • Line-interactive (VI): inverter conditions the supply, switches to battery on outage. Suitable for office-class loads, not process.
  • Standby (VFD): inverter idle, transfers on outage. Lowest cost, highest transfer-time risk; not used for process loads.

7.2Sizing Steps

  1. Tabulate UPS loads (DF = 1.00). Use kVA, not kW, since UPS rating is in kVA.
  2. Apply 25% future margin for additional loads.
  3. Select UPS rating from standard sizes: 10, 20, 30, 40, 60, 80, 100, 120, 160, 200, 300, 400, 500 kVA.
  4. Determine autonomy time (typical: 30 min for control rooms, 60 min for safety systems, 4 hr for emergency lighting; verify per SES).
  5. Calculate battery DC current at end of discharge: I_bat = (UPS kVA × 1000) / (V_bat_min × η_inv × PF_load).
  6. Size battery using IEEE 485 (VLA) or IEEE 1188 (VRLA): determine cells per string, capacity per cell, and number of parallel strings.

Chapter 8

Cable Design

Cable sizing is a three-way check — ampacity, voltage drop, and short-circuit withstand — and the largest conductor demanded by any of the three wins. This chapter covers the three criteria with their governing equations, insulation system selection, and the IEC 60364-5-52 installation methods whose ampacity tables are so frequently misapplied.

Why it matters

Cables are the most numerous electrical asset on site and the least accessible after construction. A derating factor missed at design costs a trench excavation to fix.

Learning objectives
  1. Apply the ampacity criterion with ambient, grouping, and soil derating factors.
  2. Compute voltage drop including reactance, and apply the correct limits for lighting, power, and motor circuits.
  3. Verify adiabatic short-circuit withstand using the correct k-factor for the insulation system.
  4. Select the installation method whose ampacity table actually matches the physical route.

8.1The Three Sizing Criteria

Cable sizing is governed by three independent criteria. The cable must satisfy all three; the largest cross-section among them is selected.

Criterion 1: Continuous Current Capacity

Iz ≥ Ib / (Ka × Kg × Ks), where Iz is the published ampacity, Ib is the design current (1.25 × FLA for motors per NEC 430.22), Ka is the ambient temperature derate, Kg is the grouping derate, and Ks is the soil thermal resistivity derate (for buried cables). Published ampacities follow IEC 60364-5-52, BS 7671, or NEC tables.

Criterion 2: Voltage Drop

ΔV (%) = (√3 × Ib × L × (R cos φ + X sin φ)) × 100 / VLL, where R = 22.5 / CSA (mΩ/m for Cu at 20°C; multiply by 1.20 for operating temperature 90°C XLPE) and X ≈ 0.10 mΩ/m for XLPE 3-core. Limits per IEC 60364-5-52: ≤ 3% for lighting, ≤ 5% for power, ≤ 5% from origin to motor terminals (and ≤ 10% during motor start).

Criterion 3: Short-Circuit Withstand

CSA ≥ √(Isc² × t) / k, where Isc is the prospective fault current (kA), t is the protection clearing time (s), and k = 143 for Cu/XLPE, 115 for Cu/PVC, 94 for Al/XLPE. This ensures the cable survives a fault until the upstream protection clears it.

  • Using ampacity at 30°C ambient without correcting for actual site ambient (often 50°C in buried sand or hot equipment rooms).
  • Voltage drop calculation that ignores X for long MV cables — at 6.6 kV and beyond, X dominates over R.
  • Specifying Al conductors for sizes < 25 mm² — Al cannot be reliably terminated below this size; SES prohibits.

Chapter 9

Power System Protection

Protection is the plant's immune system: it must detect every credible fault, clear it fast enough to prevent damage, trip only the smallest possible zone, and never operate when it should not. This chapter sets out the five-principle philosophy, the standard protection sets per equipment class, and the time–current coordination method with its CTI rules.

Why it matters

An uncoordinated system converts a single motor fault into a plant-wide blackout; an insensitive one lets equipment burn while every relay stays quiet. Coordination is where protection theory meets operating reality.

Learning objectives
  1. Rank and apply the five protection principles: selectivity, speed, sensitivity, reliability, simplicity.
  2. Assign the standard ANSI function sets for transformers, motors, feeders, and buses.
  3. Build and verify time–current coordination with CTI ≥ 0.3 s and cable damage-curve clearance.

9.1Protection Philosophy

Power system protection rests on five principles, in order of priority:

  1. Selectivity (coordination): only the device closest to the fault should trip — never a device upstream of the fault when a downstream device could clear it.
  2. Speed: faults must be cleared before equipment damage thresholds are reached (typically 5 cycles for transformer differentials, 100 ms for downstream feeders, 300 ms for backup zones).
  3. Sensitivity: the relay must detect faults at the far end of its protected zone with adequate margin (typical pickup at ≥ 1.5× minimum fault, or detect ≥ 2× nominal load for overload).
  4. Reliability (dependability + security): the relay must operate when required (dependability) and must not operate when not required (security). These two are often in tension.
  5. Simplicity: the simplest scheme that meets the above wins; complex schemes are harder to commission, test, and maintain.

9.2Protection Functions by Equipment Class

Transformer Protection

  • 87T — differential, primary protection for transformers ≥ 5 MVA.
  • 51 — overcurrent on primary, backup for 87T and protection for through faults.
  • 51N — restricted earth fault on the star winding.
  • 49T — top-oil and winding temperature.
  • 63 — Buchholz / sudden pressure (oil-filled transformers).
  • 26 — winding temperature trip (typically 110°C alarm, 130°C trip).

Motor Protection

  • 49 — thermal overload (replica of motor heating curve).
  • 50/51 — instantaneous and time overcurrent (short-circuit and locked-rotor).
  • 46 — negative-sequence (single-phasing and unbalance).
  • 66 — start counter (limits starts per hour).
  • 48 — incomplete sequence (motor failed to accelerate).
  • 87M — differential (motors ≥ 1500 kW or critical service).
  • 64 — earth fault (resistance-grounded systems).

Feeder Protection

  • 50/51 — phase overcurrent.
  • 50N/51N or 50G/51G — ground overcurrent.
  • 67 — directional (parallel feeders, ring buses).
  • 79 — auto-reclose (overhead lines only; never on cables).

Bus Protection

  • 87B — bus differential, ≥ 30 kA bus, primary protection for high-fault buses.
  • Zone interlocking — alternative to 87B for budget projects (slower).

9.3Coordination Curves

Time-current coordination is built by plotting the inverse-time curves of upstream and downstream devices on a log-log graph and verifying minimum coordination time intervals (CTI):

  • CTI between two relays: ≥ 0.30 s (allowing for relay operating time, breaker time, and safety margin).
  • CTI between a relay and a downstream fuse: ≥ 0.30 s.
  • CTI between two fuses: confirm by manufacturer total-clearing vs. minimum-melting curves with ≥ 25% margin in current.

Standard inverse curves per IEC 60255-151: Standard Inverse (SI), Very Inverse (VI), Extremely Inverse (EI), Long-Time Inverse (LTI). EI is preferred for transformer feeder protection because it coordinates better with downstream fuses; SI is general-purpose.

9.4Common Pitfalls

  • Setting instantaneous (50) too low — causes trip on inrush or motor starting.
  • Setting overcurrent pickup too high — does not detect end-of-zone faults.
  • Forgetting to coordinate with downstream fuses — fuse should clear first for downstream faults.
  • Using the same CT for protection and metering — protection CTs (5P or 10P) saturate at a known multiple of rated; metering CTs (Class 0.5) saturate early and lose accuracy in fault — they are different CTs.
  • Setting differential restraint too low — relay operates on through-fault CT errors.

Chapter 10

Power System Studies (ETAP)

Power system studies are how a design proves itself before energisation. The six standard studies — load flow, short circuit, motor starting, coordination, arc flash, and harmonics — form a sequential chain in which each study's output is the next study's input. This chapter covers the acceptance criteria, modelling practices, and reporting discipline SES expects.

Why it matters

A study built on wrong input data produces confidently wrong answers. Modelling discipline — verified impedances, motor contributions, modular validation — is what separates a study from a simulation exercise.

Learning objectives
  1. State the purpose and acceptance criteria of each of the six standard studies.
  2. Apply the modelling best practices that keep results defensible: test impedances, motor contribution, modular verification.
  3. Structure study reports with tracked exceptions and cross-consistent assumptions.

10.1The Six Standard Studies

Every greenfield substation and every major brownfield modification at SABIC requires the following studies. They are interdependent: the output of one feeds the input of the next.

Load Flow

Determines steady-state bus voltages, line loadings, and transformer loadings under normal and contingency conditions. Acceptance criteria: bus voltages within ±5% of nominal, equipment loadings ≤ 100% of rating, no transformers overloaded under N-1 contingency.

Short-Circuit

Calculates three-phase, line-to-ground, line-to-line, and line-to-line-to-ground fault currents at every bus. Per IEC 60909. Output drives switchgear interrupting ratings, cable SC sizing, and protection setting calculations. Verify that breaker Icu ≥ calculated Isc with ≥ 10% margin.

Motor Starting

Dynamic simulation of the largest motor start (typically firewater pump or main process compressor). Output: bus voltage profile during start, motor accelerating torque vs. load torque, time to full speed. Acceptance: bus voltage ≥ 80% throughout, motor reaches rated speed within manufacturer's allowable time.

Harmonic Analysis

Per IEEE 519. Calculates voltage and current harmonics at the point of common coupling (PCC). Acceptance: total voltage distortion (THD-V) ≤ 5%, individual harmonic ≤ 3%; current distortion limits depend on the short-circuit ratio at the PCC. Drives the specification of harmonic filters and capacitor sizing.

Arc Flash

Per IEEE 1584. Calculates incident energy (cal/cm²) at every bus and determines required PPE category. Outputs feed labeling, work practices, and equipment specification (arc-resistant switchgear).

Protection Coordination

Time-current curves overlaid for all protection devices in series. Verifies CTI is met for all foreseeable fault locations. Often combined with arc flash to confirm that fast-clearing settings reduce incident energy adequately.

10.2Modeling Best Practices

  • Use manufacturer test data for transformer impedance, not nameplate — test certificate gives Z at the actual test temperature.
  • Model utility source as ideal voltage behind subtransient reactance, with X/R from utility data.
  • Include all running motors in short-circuit calculation — motor SC contribution is real and is approximately 4–6× FLA.
  • Build the model in modules (substation by substation) and verify each before connecting to the next.
  • Always run the load flow first; if voltages are unrealistic, the input data is wrong, and every other study built on it will be wrong.

10.3Reporting

  • Each study report should contain: scope, assumptions, single-line diagram, input data tables, output summary, exceptions and recommendations, and conclusions.
  • Cross-reference between studies — load flow assumptions must match short-circuit assumptions, etc.
  • Track exceptions formally — every red flag from the study must be either resolved (design change) or accepted (with justification documented).

10.4Common Pitfalls

  • Using nameplate impedance instead of test impedance for the controlling transformers.
  • Forgetting motor contribution to short-circuit — particularly for buses with many motors.
  • Running motor starting study on a snapshot only — should run for full acceleration time to confirm voltage recovery.
  • Specifying only THD-V without also checking individual harmonic limits — IEEE 519 has both.
  • Accepting arc flash labels in the engineering phase without verifying actual protection settings will be implemented as modeled — protection setting changes during commissioning invalidate the arc flash study.

  • Run the studies in sequence — load flow first; if the voltages are unrealistic, everything downstream is wrong.
  • Use test-certificate impedances for the controlling transformers, not nameplate values.
  • Motor short-circuit contribution is real: 4–6 × FLA for the first cycles at every motor-heavy bus.

Chapter 11

Hazardous Area Installation

In a petrochemical plant, ordinary electrical equipment in the wrong place is an ignition source. Hazardous-area engineering per IEC 60079 layers three concepts — zone, gas group, and temperature class — and matches every piece of equipment to the atmosphere it will live in. This chapter covers the classification framework, protection concepts, equipment marking, cable glands, and the documentation trail that makes it all auditable.

Why it matters

Area classification errors are invisible until the day a release meets an unprotected enclosure. The discipline is documentary as much as technical: the verification dossier is what proves the installation safe.

Learning objectives
  1. Classify areas into Zones 0/1/2 and map equipment categories to each.
  2. Decode Ex protection concepts (d, e, i, n, p) and read a full equipment marking string.
  3. Select certified cable glands and entries matching the protection concept.
  4. Maintain the documentation set: classification drawings, equipment schedule, and inspection records per IEC 60079-17.

11.1Area Classification Framework

Hazardous area design follows IEC 60079 (in SABIC service) with three layered concepts: zone, group, and temperature class. The combination dictates which equipment can be installed where.

Zones (Gas — IEC 60079-10-1)

  • Zone 0: explosive atmosphere is present continuously, or for long periods (inside a fuel storage tank vapor space).
  • Zone 1: explosive atmosphere is likely to occur in normal operation occasionally (around a vent, near a flange that may leak).
  • Zone 2: explosive atmosphere is not likely in normal operation, and if it does occur, will exist only for a short period (general process area, well-ventilated indoor unit).

Zones (Dust — IEC 60079-10-2)

  • Zone 20: dust cloud present continuously or frequently.
  • Zone 21: dust cloud likely in normal operation.
  • Zone 22: dust cloud not likely in normal operation.

Gas Groups

  • IIA: propane, ethanol — easiest to contain.
  • IIB: ethylene — intermediate.
  • IIC: hydrogen, acetylene — most demanding (smallest MESG, lowest MIE).

Temperature Classes

The maximum surface temperature of the equipment must be below the auto-ignition temperature of the surrounding gas, with a margin:

  • T1 ≤ 450°C, T2 ≤ 300°C, T3 ≤ 200°C, T4 ≤ 135°C, T5 ≤ 100°C, T6 ≤ 85°C.

11.3Equipment Marking — Reading the Label

A typical motor label reads: Ex db IIB T4 Gb. Decoded:

  • Ex: certified explosion-protected equipment.
  • db: protection concept = flameproof, equipment protection level (EPL) Gb (suitable for Zone 1).
  • IIB: gas group — suitable for IIA and IIB gases, NOT IIC.
  • T4: maximum surface temperature ≤ 135°C.
  • Gb: gas atmosphere, EPL b (Zone 1 and 2).

11.4Cable Glands and Entries

Cable entries are the most common point of failure in hazardous area installations. Rules:

  • Glands must be certified for the same protection concept and gas group as the enclosure (Ex d or Ex e barrier glands).
  • For Ex d enclosures with inflammable gas inside (or directly connected to a Zone 0 source), use barrier glands with internal compound seal.
  • For Ex e enclosures, standard certified glands are acceptable; thread engagement must be ≥ 5 full threads.
  • Unused entries must be plugged with certified blanking elements — not bolts, not tape.
  • Cable glands must be tightened to manufacturer torque — over-tightening can crack the gland body, under-tightening loses the seal.

11.5Documentation Requirements

  • Hazardous area classification drawing — a plan view of the unit showing zone boundaries with elevation views where vertical extent matters.
  • Equipment register / Ex inventory — every item of Ex equipment with manufacturer, model, certificate number, gas group, T-class, zone.
  • Verification dossier per IEC 60079-14 — proves each item is suitable for its installed location.
  • Inspection records per IEC 60079-17 — initial, periodic, and continuous inspection.

11.6Common Pitfalls

  • Installing IIB-rated equipment in a IIC area (e.g., near hydrogen lines) — violates the gas group requirement.
  • Using standard glands on Ex d enclosures — barrier glands are required.
  • Forgetting to update the area classification drawing after process changes — vent relocations and new tank lines change the zones.
  • Mixing intrinsically safe (Ex i) and non-IS circuits in the same multi-core cable without a certified barrier.
  • Using non-Ex equipment temporarily during construction — area is hazardous regardless of construction status.

Key Takeaways — Chapter 11
  • Zone tells you how often the atmosphere is present; group and temperature class tell you which equipment survives it.
  • The weakest element rules: an Ex d enclosure with an ordinary gland is not Ex d.
  • T-class must sit below the auto-ignition temperature of the actual gas mixture — verify against the plant's specific chemistry.
  • The verification dossier (drawings, schedule, certificates, inspections) is a living document — an installation without it fails audit regardless of hardware quality.
